Speaker Notes — Formality & Honorifics in Kazakh

— Title Slide

Hi everyone, I'm Austin Wagner, and today I'll be presenting on formality and honorifics in Kazakh — specifically the sen/siz pronoun system — and how it compares to Japanese keigo. We'll be looking at how two completely unrelated languages, from completely different parts of the world, both developed systems for encoding respect and social hierarchy directly into their grammar.

"So why did I pick this topic?"

01 Why This Topic?

Throughout this course, we've studied how Japanese speakers navigate social relationships through keigo — the three-level honorific system with sonkeigo, kenjoogo, and teineigo. Outside of this class, I've been doing research on Turkic languages at UC Irvine. I co-authored a paper on mutual intelligibility across six Turkic languages using character-level language models, which was accepted at HSP 2026 and Tu+11 at MIT.

Through that research, I became familiar with Kazakh and noticed that it has a pronoun-based formality system that serves a very similar function to Japanese keigo. Speakers have to choose between "sen," the informal "you," and "siz," the formal "you," and that choice affects the entire sentence. I found it interesting that two typologically unrelated languages are answering the same fundamental question: how do you encode respect and social hierarchy through speech?

"Let's start with the basics of the Kazakh system."

02 Сен vs. Сіз

Many languages split the word "you" into two forms — one informal, one formal. Linguists call this the T-V distinction, a term from Brown and Gilman in 1960. The name comes from Latin "tu" and "vos." You see it in French with tu and vous, in Spanish with tú and usted, in German with du and Sie, and in Russian with ty and vy. Kazakh's version is sen and siz — sen is informal, siz is formal.

In Kazakh, the choice between sen and siz is mandatory in every interaction. You use sen with friends, peers, younger people, and close family. You use siz with elders, strangers, bosses, teachers, and officials. The key thing is that age hierarchy overrides closeness — a younger person speaking to an older relative always uses siz, even within the family. This is very different from Japanese, where family members typically use casual speech with each other. In Kazakh, formality is mandatory regardless of how close you are.

"And it's not just the pronoun that changes — the whole verb does too."

03 Verb Endings Change Too

When you switch between sen and siz, the entire verb conjugation shifts to match. Both Kazakh and Turkish are agglutinative languages, which means you build words by stacking suffixes onto a root. So the formality marker is literally attached to the verb as an ending.

For example, "How are you?" informally is "Sen qalaisyn" — with the suffix -syn on the verb. The formal version is "Siz qalaisyz" — same root, but now the suffix is -syz. The verb ending must agree with the pronoun. You can't mix them. And it goes beyond just the verb — even "with you" changes: senimen is informal, sizben is formal. The whole sentence bends around the formality choice.

Here's another interesting thing: both Kazakh and Japanese are pro-drop languages, meaning speakers often omit the pronoun entirely. But even when you drop it, the verb ending in Kazakh still reveals which formality level you chose. You literally cannot hide the choice.

Japanese has a parallel pattern. Casually you say "genki?" which just means "well?" or "are you well?" But the polite version is "ogenki desu ka?" — it adds a respect prefix, a formal copula meaning "is," and a question particle. So in both languages, going formal doesn't just change one word — it restructures the whole sentence.

"Beyond pronouns and verbs, both languages also use honorific titles."

04 Honorific Titles

Kazakh has dedicated titles for showing respect. Aga — spelled A-G-A — means a respected older male. Apay — A-P-A-Y — means a respected older female. And Qurmetti means "respected" and is used in formal speeches and letters.

Compare this to Japanese, where you have -san as a general polite suffix, like saying Mr. or Ms. Then -sama for very formal situations or addressing customers. And -sensei for teachers or doctors. Both languages require speakers to mark respect through forms of address, not just pronouns. One difference worth noting is that Kazakh titles are standalone words placed before names, while Japanese honorifics are suffixes attached after names.

"Now here's something unique to Kazakh that Japanese doesn't have."

05 Сенделесейік — Permission to Be Informal

This is one of the most interesting differences between the two languages. In Kazakh, when two strangers meet, they default to siz — formality is the safe starting point. As they get to know each other, the older or higher-status person may say "sendeleseyik" — pronounced "sen-deh-leh-say-YIK" — which literally means "let's use sen with each other." It's an explicit, spoken invitation to drop formality.

What's important is that only the elder or the person of higher status can initiate this. The younger person never presumes to make the switch on their own. It's a real social ritual — a specific moment that marks a change in the relationship.

Japanese has no equivalent of this. The shift from keigo to casual speech happens gradually over time, without any single defining moment. In Kazakh, the formality boundary is visible and negotiable — there's a word for crossing it. In Japanese, it's more of a slow fade.

"So what happens if you don't follow these rules?"

06 What Happens When You Get It Wrong

In Kazakh, using sen with a stranger or an elder can't be walked back. The offense is in the pronoun itself, not in your tone. It's a binary mistake — you picked the wrong form, and that's it. In Japanese, the error is more gradient — you might use a politeness level that's close but not quite right, and the severity depends on how far off you were.

In a business context, the consequences are real. Japanese companies actually train their employees specifically on correct keigo usage because getting it wrong with a client can damage or even cost a business relationship. In Kazakh, using sen with someone you should be addressing as siz can genuinely insult them.

In both cases, the consequences are social, not grammatical. The sentence is still technically correct — the grammar works fine — but the relationship is damaged. Both systems punish you socially for making the wrong register choice.

"Let's put the two systems side by side."

07 Kazakh vs. Japanese — Side by Side

So the T-V distinction — that framework from Brown and Gilman — describes languages that split "you" into informal and formal, like French tu/vous, Spanish tú/usted, or Kazakh sen/siz. Japanese takes a fundamentally different approach. It doesn't split the pronoun at all — instead, it builds formality into the verb system and vocabulary.

Let me walk through the key differences. In Kazakh, formality lives in the pronoun and the verb ending. In Japanese, it lives in verb forms, vocabulary, and prefixes. Kazakh has two levels — informal or formal, nothing in between. Japanese has at least three — teineigo for general politeness, kenjoogo for humbling yourself, and sonkeigo for elevating the person you're talking to or about.

What determines the choice is also different. In Kazakh, it's based on age and status, and those boundaries are mostly fixed for life. In Japanese, it's based on uchi/soto — whether someone is in your in-group or out-group — and that can shift depending on context and audience. The same person might be uchi in one conversation and soto in another.

The switching mechanism is different too. In Kazakh, there's an explicit ritual — sendeleseyik — initiated by the elder. In Japanese, the shift is gradual, with no single defining moment. And family dynamics are opposite: in Kazakh, you still use siz with older relatives because age always outranks closeness. In Japanese, casual speech at home is normal — intimacy overrides hierarchy.

Both languages are also pro-drop — speakers often omit the pronoun — but in both cases, the verb form or verb ending still reveals your formality level. You can't hide it in either language.

There's also a video here showing Turkish, which is another Turkic language with the exact same sen/siz system. This shows the pattern isn't unique to Kazakh — it's widespread across the Turkic language family.

"So what's the big takeaway?"

08 What This Comparison Reveals

Kazakh and Japanese are typologically unrelated. They come from different language families, different regions of the world, and they have no historical contact with each other. Yet both independently developed grammatical systems that force speakers to calculate social position before every sentence they say.

Kazakh chose the pronoun as the carrier of respect — a single binary choice between sen and siz that reshapes the whole sentence, including verb endings and even prepositions. Japanese chose the verb and vocabulary — a multi-layered system where the same idea can be expressed in dozens of different ways depending on who you're talking to, who you're talking about, and who's listening.

What this suggests is that encoding respect into grammar isn't a quirk of one particular culture. It's a recurring human solution to the problem of navigating social hierarchy through speech. Two completely unrelated languages arrived at the same conclusion — that respect shouldn't just be felt, it should be spoken — and each built its own grammatical tools to make that happen.
