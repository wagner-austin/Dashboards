Speaker Notes — Formality & Honorifics in Kazakh

01 Title Slide

  • Introduce yourself: "Hi, I'm Austin Wagner, and today I'll be presenting on formality and honorifics in Kazakh — specifically the sen/siz pronoun system — and comparing it to Japanese keigo."
  • Frame the scope: "We'll look at how two completely unrelated languages both encode respect through speech."
  • Mention both flags on screen — Kazakhstan and Japan.
Transition: "So why did I pick this topic?"

02 Why This Topic?

  • This course covers Japanese keigo — three levels of honorific speech.
  • Outside this class, I do research on Turkic languages at UCI.
  • I co-authored a paper on mutual intelligibility across six Turkic languages using character-level language models — accepted at HSP 2026 and Tu+11 at MIT.
  • Through that research, I became familiar with Kazakh and noticed its pronoun-based formality system parallels keigo.
  • The core question both languages answer: how do you encode respect and social hierarchy through speech?
Transition: "Let's start with the basics of the Kazakh system."

03 Сен vs. Сіз

  • Kazakh has a binary pronoun system — two forms of "you."
  • Сен (sen) = informal — friends, peers, younger people, close family.
  • Сіз (siz) = formal — elders, strangers, bosses, teachers, officials.
  • This is called the T-V distinction — a term from Brown & Gilman (1960). Named after French tu/vous. Found in many languages: Spanish tú/usted, German du/Sie, Russian ты/вы.
  • Key point: the choice is mandatory in every interaction — you cannot avoid it.
  • Age hierarchy overrides closeness — a younger person uses сіз with an older relative, even within the family. This is different from Japanese, where family members typically use casual speech.
  • Using the wrong form has real social consequences — it's not just grammar, it's a social statement.
Transition: "And it's not just the pronoun that changes — the whole verb does too."

04 Verb Endings Change Too

  • When you switch pronouns, the entire verb conjugation shifts.
  • Informal: "Sen qalaisyn?" — "How are you?" with the -сын (-syn) ending.
  • Formal: "Сіз қалайсыз?" — same question, but -сыз (-syz) ending.
  • The verb ending must agree with the pronoun — it's not optional.
  • Japanese has the same pattern: casual 元気？ (genki?) vs. polite お元気ですか？ (ogenki desu ka?) — adds the prefix お, the copula です, and question particle か.
  • Takeaway: in both languages, formality restructures the whole sentence, not just one word.
Transition: "Beyond pronouns and verbs, both languages also use honorific titles."

05 Honorific Titles

  • Kazakh has dedicated titles for showing respect:
  • Аға (Aga) — respected older male.
  • Апай (Apay) — respected older female.
  • Құрметті (Qurmetti) — means "respected," used in formal speeches and letters.
  • Compare to Japanese: -san (general polite), -sama (very formal, customers), -sensei (teacher, doctor).
  • Both languages require speakers to mark respect through forms of address, not just pronouns.
  • Note the difference: Kazakh titles are standalone words placed before names, while Japanese uses suffixes attached after names.
Transition: "Now here's something unique to Kazakh that Japanese doesn't have."

06 Сенделесейік — Permission to Be Informal

  • This is one of the most interesting differences.
  • When two strangers meet, they default to сіз — formality is the safe starting point.
  • As they get closer, the older or higher-status person may say: "Сенделесейік" — literally "Let's use sen with each other."
  • This is an explicit, spoken social ritual — a specific moment that marks a change in the relationship.
  • Only the elder/superior can initiate — the younger person never presumes.
  • Japanese has no equivalent. The shift from keigo to casual speech happens gradually, without a single defining moment.
  • This is a great example of how Kazakh makes the formality boundary visible and negotiable.
Transition: "So what happens if you don't follow these rules?"

07 What Happens When You Get It Wrong

  • In Kazakh: using сен with a stranger or elder is considered disrespectful — it can genuinely insult someone.
  • Example: saying "Sen qalaisyn?" to an elder instead of "Siz qalaisyz?" — the pronoun itself carries social weight.
  • In Japanese: using the plain form "iku" (行く) instead of the honorific "irasshaimasu" (いらっしゃいます) with a superior signals rudeness.
  • The consequences are social, not grammatical — people judge you by your register choice.
  • In both languages: your word choice reveals how you see the social hierarchy.
Transition: "Let's put the two systems side by side."

08 Kazakh vs. Japanese — Side by Side

  • Kazakh has a binary system — two levels: сен (informal) and сіз (formal).
  • Japanese has a multi-layered system — three levels: 丁寧語 teineigo (polite), 謙譲語 kenjōgo (humble), 尊敬語 sonkeigo (respectful).
  • Kazakh formality is based on age, status, and closeness — boundaries are mostly fixed.
  • Japanese keigo is based on uchi/soto (in-group/out-group) and social rank — boundaries shift depending on context and audience.
  • Kazakh builds formality into pronouns and verb endings.
  • Japanese builds formality into verb forms, vocabulary, and entire speech levels.
  • The diagram shows this visually: Kazakh = two blocks, Japanese = three graduated blocks.
Transition: "So what's the big takeaway?"

09 Conclusion — Different Tools, Same Goal

  • Both languages require speakers to make social calculations before every utterance.
  • Kazakh uses a binary pronoun system — the boundaries are fixed by age and status, and they rarely shift.
  • Japanese uses a three-level keigo system — sonkeigo raises others, kenjōgo humbles yourself, teineigo is general politeness. Boundaries shift with context and audience (uchi vs. soto).
  • Despite being typologically unrelated, both languages reflect the same principle: respect is not just felt, it is spoken.
  • Each culture developed its own grammatical tools to make social hierarchy visible in everyday conversation.
  • End strong: "Different structures, same goal — and that's what makes cross-linguistic comparison so valuable."
