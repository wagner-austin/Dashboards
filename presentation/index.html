Formality & Honorifics in Kazakh
The Sen/Siz System

Compared to Japanese Keigo

Austin Wagner LSCI 169 — Japanese Sociolinguistics UC Irvine — Winter 2026
Motivation

01 Why This Topic?

Connecting Japanese sociolinguistics to Turkic language research.

  • This course covers Japanese keigo — three levels of honorific speech
  • I also research Turkic languages at UCI, including a paper on mutual intelligibility across six Turkic languages (accepted at HSP 2026 and Tu+11 at MIT)
  • Through that work, I noticed Kazakh has a pronoun-based formality system (сен/сіз) that parallels keigo
  • Two unrelated languages, same question: how do you encode respect through speech?
Kazakh Pronouns

02 Сен vs. Сіз

Many languages split "you" into informal and formal — linguists call this the T-V distinction (Brown & Gilman, 1960), named after Latin tu/vos. French has tu/vous, Spanish has tú/usted, German has du/Sie. Kazakh's version is sen/siz.

  • The choice is based on age, social status, and closeness — and it's mandatory in every interaction
  • A younger person speaking to an older relative always uses сіз, even within the family — age hierarchy overrides closeness
  • In Japanese, family members typically use casual speech — in Kazakh, formality is mandatory regardless of intimacy
  • Using the wrong form has real social consequences — it's not just grammar, it's a social statement

Grammar

03 Verb Endings Change Too

It's not just the pronoun — the entire verb conjugation shifts.

Japanese — Parallel structure
Casual:  "genki?" (元気？) — "Are you well?"
Polite:   "ogenki desu ka?" (お元気ですか？) — "Are you well?" (polite) — teineigo (丁寧語 = polite speech)
  • The verb ending changes from -сын (-syn) to -сыз (-syz) to match the formality of the pronoun
  • It goes further than verbs — even "with you" changes: сенімен (senimen, informal) vs. сізбен (sizben, formal). The whole sentence bends around the formality choice
  • Both Kazakh and Japanese are pro-drop — speakers often omit the pronoun entirely. But in Kazakh, the verb ending (-syn/-syz) still reveals your formality level. You can't hide the choice
  • Japanese parallel: casual "genki?" vs. polite "ogenki desu ka?" — polite form adds a respect prefix, a formal copula ("is"), and a question particle

Honorifics

04 Honorific Titles

Both languages require speakers to mark respect through forms of address.

Social Ritual

05 Сенделесейік — Permission to Be Informal

An explicit invitation to drop formality — unique to Kazakh.

  • When two strangers meet, they default to сіз — formality is the safe starting point
  • The older or higher-status person initiates the switch — it's never presumed by the younger person
  • This is an explicit, spoken moment — a social ritual that marks a change in the relationship
  • Japanese has no equivalent — the shift from keigo to casual happens gradually, without a single defining moment
[4] Amanbaeva. "National Features of Speech Etiquette." Azbuka.kz. — The сенделесейік ritual.
Social Weight

06 What Happens When You Get It Wrong

The words you choose reveal how you see the relationship.

  • In Kazakh, using sen with an elder cannot be walked back — the offense is in the pronoun itself, not the tone
  • In Japanese, the wrong register with a client can cost a business deal — companies train employees specifically on keigo
  • Key difference: Kazakh errors are binary (you picked the wrong pronoun) — Japanese errors are gradient (you used the wrong level of politeness)
  • Both systems punish socially, not grammatically — the sentence is still "correct," but the relationship is damaged

Analysis

07 Kazakh vs. Japanese — Side by Side

The T-V distinction (Brown & Gilman, 1960) describes languages with two pronouns for "you" — informal (T) and formal (V), like French tu/vous, Spanish tú/usted, or Kazakh sen/siz. Japanese takes a fundamentally different approach — no pronoun switch at all.

Kazakh — T-V System

  • Where formality lives: in the pronoun (sen/siz) and verb ending (-syn/-syz)
  • How many levels: two — informal or formal, nothing in between
  • What determines it: age and status, mostly fixed for life
  • How it switches: explicit ritual (сенделесейік), initiated by the elder
  • Family speech: siz required with older relatives — age always outranks closeness
  • Pronoun dropping: Kazakh is pro-drop — you can omit the pronoun, but the verb ending still exposes your formality choice

Japanese — Keigo System

  • Where formality lives: in verb forms, vocabulary, and prefixes — not pronouns
  • How many levels: three — teineigo (polite), kenjōgo (humble self), sonkeigo (elevate others)
  • What determines it: uchi/soto (in-group vs. out-group), shifts by context
  • How it switches: gradual — no single moment, just slowly becomes more casual
  • Family speech: casual is normal — intimacy overrides hierarchy at home
  • Pronoun dropping: also pro-drop — pronouns are rarely used, but the verb form always signals your register

Conclusion

08 What This Comparison Reveals

Two unrelated languages, two different mechanisms, one shared insight.

  • Kazakh and Japanese are typologically unrelated — different language families, different regions, no historical contact
  • Yet both independently developed grammatical systems that force speakers to calculate social position before every sentence
  • Kazakh chose the pronoun as the carrier of respect — a single binary choice that reshapes the whole sentence
  • Japanese chose the verb and vocabulary — a multi-layered system where the same idea can be said dozens of different ways depending on who's listening
  • This suggests that encoding respect in grammar isn't a quirk of one culture — it's a recurring human solution to the problem of navigating social hierarchy through speech
